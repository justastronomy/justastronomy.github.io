Why do dark skies
matter for astronomy?

Light pollution — the brightening of the night sky by artificial lights — has quietly erased the stars for most of humanity. Over 80% of the world's population now lives under light-polluted skies. In India, hundreds of millions of people living in or near cities may never witness the Milky Way in their lifetimes.

For astronomers, a dark sky is not a preference — it is a prerequisite. At a truly dark site you can see thousands of stars, like diamonds strewn acrosss the sky, trace the full arc of the Milky Way, and spot faint nebulae and galaxies, all with the naked eye. That same sky from a typical Indian city? Fewer than 200 stars, the Milky Way entirely invisible, and a perpetual orange glow overhead.

Travelling even 150–200 km from a major city can jump you three or four Bortle classes — the difference between frustration and wonder.

✦ The Bortle Scale

Developed by amateur astronomer John E. Bortle in 2001, the Bortle Dark-Sky Scale is a nine-level numeric measure of the darkness of the night sky at any location. Class 1 is the darkest sky on Earth — found only in extremely remote, high-altitude deserts and wilderness. Class 9 is the washed-out inner-city sky where only the Moon and a handful of the brightest stars cut through the orange glow.

The scale considers the visibility of faint objects, the darkness of the sky background, and the degree of light-pollution glow on the horizon — giving observers a practical, standardised way to assess a site before they travel.
